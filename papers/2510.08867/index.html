arXiv:2510.08867

ReviewerToo: Should AI Join The Program Committee?

The whole paper in 5 minutes 🧠💥

ICLR 2025 peer-review data 1,963 sampled submissions gpt-oss-120b agents Hybrid review, not autopilot

The story

Peer review is overloaded. Top machine learning conferences get huge waves of papers, and the old system depends on tired humans making hard calls under time pressure.

📚 Too many papers arrive at once.
😵 Reviewers disagree, rush, or focus on different things.
⚖️ The final decision still needs judgment, not just paperwork.
Imagine a giant cooking contest where every judge tastes a different bite, uses a slightly different scorecard, and has to finish before midnight. ReviewerToo asks: can a careful robot prep cook help the judges without choosing the winner alone?

ReviewerToo is a pretend program committee made of AI agents. One agent reads nearby research, several agents write reviews from different personalities, an author agent writes a rebuttal, and a meta-reviewer agent tries to combine everything into one final recommendation.

🔎 One agent gathers the surrounding literature.
🧑‍⚖️ Different reviewer agents act strict, generous, experimental, theoretical, or clarity-focused.
🧾 A meta-reviewer agent weighs the pile and tries to make the final call.
It is like asking five friends to review the same movie: one cares about plot holes, one cares about acting, one cares about camera work, and one just wants it to be fun. The point is not that any one friend is right. The point is that the group exposes blind spots.

The paper found that the AI committee got surprisingly close on simple accept-versus-reject calls. It still struggled with finer labels like "top-tier accept" versus "ordinary accept", but the combined meta-reviewer was much stronger than most single AI reviewers.

✅ The combined AI reviewer was useful for broad triage.
🤏 Fine-grained prestige tiers were much harder.
🧩 Combining reviewers helped more than trusting one personality.
The AI is better at sorting laundry into "clean enough" and "needs washing" than deciding whether a shirt belongs in a designer boutique. Broad buckets: decent. Subtle taste calls: still human territory.

The most important warning is that AI can become too agreeable after a rebuttal. When the simulated authors defended the paper, some AI reviewers became more likely to accept, even when the ground truth said reject.

🙃 Rebuttals can charm the model.
🚨 More accepting does not always mean more accurate.
🧑‍🔬 Human area chairs still need to supervise the final judgment.
Think of a student explaining why their late homework deserves full credit. A kind teacher may soften. A good system has to listen, but also remember the rubric.

TL;DR

ReviewerToo says AI can raise the floor of peer review by giving structured, consistent, multi-perspective feedback, but it should sit beside human reviewers - not replace the program committee.

The Question

The paper asks whether large language model agents can participate responsibly in peer review: not as a one-shot review generator, but as a structured system with roles, evidence, rebuttal, aggregation, and evaluation against real conference outcomes.

  • pressure

    Large conferences receive many thousands of submissions, while human reviewer attention is scarce and uneven.

  • risk

    One-off AI deployments can be anecdotal, hard to reproduce, and too easy to mistake for real judgment.

  • proposal

    ReviewerToo turns peer review into a controlled multi-agent experiment that can be measured, audited, and adapted.

11,672 ICLR 2025 submissions in the source pool.
1,963 Submissions sampled into the ICLR-2k benchmark.
5 Decision labels: oral, spotlight, poster, reject, desk reject.
81.8% Binary accuracy for the Meta (all) agent.

Key Concepts

The paper is easier to read if these four ideas stay separate.

Method

ReviewerToo turns one submitted manuscript into a sequence of evidence objects, reviewer stances, rebuttal claims, and a calibrated final suggestion.

Paper ingestion

The submitted paper is converted into a machine-readable manuscript while the real conference decision remains the held-out ground truth.

Surface layer A full paper submission with title, abstract, method, experiments, figures, and claims.
Data layer Markdown text plus rubric fields, hidden labels, and later model-generated review artifacts.

Results

The strongest headline is not that one AI reviewer wins. It is that aggregation closes much of the gap on the broad accept/reject task while preserving structured feedback.

2-way accuracy

What Changed After Rebuttal?

The rebuttal round is the paper's sharpest cautionary tale. It adds realistic workflow structure, but the models may over-credit author defenses.

Before rebuttal: evidence-first reviewer

The empiricist reviewer with conference instructions and LitLLM had the strongest single-agent ELO in the ablation table.

70.7 Binary F1 before rebuttal.
1558 Review-quality ELO before rebuttal.

After rebuttal: more accepting, less aligned

When rebuttal was included, false positives rose and the same configuration fell on both F1 and ELO.

60.4 Binary F1 after rebuttal.
1332 Review-quality ELO after rebuttal.

Implications

The paper's recommendations are design constraints for a hybrid review system.

For program chairs

Use AI as a consistency and coverage layer: literature checks, rubric completeness, missing-baseline flags, and structured summaries.

For reviewers

Treat model reviews as a second pass that can surface blind spots, not as a source of final authority.

For tool builders

Optimize for review quality, calibration, and traceable evidence, not just for matching accept/reject labels.

Limits

ReviewerToo is useful because it exposes limits as much as successes.

calibration

Five-way decisions remain hard, especially distinctions among oral, spotlight, and poster acceptances.

judge

Review quality depends on an LLM-as-judge ELO protocol; this is informative but not the same as broad human validation.

rebuttal

Author responses can push agents toward acceptance, so rebuttal handling needs guardrails and calibration checks.

scope

The data comes from public OpenReview ICLR 2025 submissions; behavior may differ in other fields, venues, or private-review settings.

ethics

The authors explicitly frame the system as research and controlled integration, not unsupervised automated decision-making.

Quiz

Three checks that reward the actual paper, not the vibe.

1. What was the best-performing ReviewerToo decision configuration in the main table?

Meta (all) is the important one: it reached 81.8% binary accuracy and the highest ELO in the main table.

2. Why does the paper use multiple personas?

The personas make bias and disagreement visible. The system then tests whether aggregation can stabilize those differences.

3. What is the major rebuttal-round warning?

The authors connect post-rebuttal performance drops to possible sycophancy: the model listens, but may listen too generously.
