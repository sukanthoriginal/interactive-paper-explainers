Where in your brain does "relaxed" live?

For nearly 100 years, alpha waves have been the popular signature of a calm mind. A meta-analysis of 54 studies finds the truth is location-dependent — and there may not be a single "relaxation signal" at all.

Sugimoto, Kurashiki, Xu, Takemi & Amano (2025) · JoCN 38(3) · Systematic Review & Meta-analysis

The Question

Is there a brain wave that says "I'm relaxed"?

In 1929, the German psychiatrist Hans Berger placed electrodes on a human scalp for the first time and noticed something striking: a smooth, rhythmic wave appeared when his subjects closed their eyes and went still. He named it alpha. Within a decade, alpha had a folk meaning — it was the brain's at-rest signal, the EEG equivalent of a sigh of relief.

That story stuck. Today, alpha (8–13 Hz) shows up in consumer meditation headbands, neurofeedback apps, "relaxation training" studies, and most popular accounts of how the brain works. But there's a quiet problem: the empirical evidence linking alpha to relaxation has always been inconclusive. Different studies measure different parts of the scalp, define "relaxation" differently — heart rate, questionnaires, skin sweat — and reach different conclusions. Nobody had pulled it all together.

What this paper does: A formal PRISMA-guided systematic review of every EEG-and-relaxation study published since 1940. 4,194 papers screened, 54 included for qualitative synthesis, 31 entered the meta-analysis — pooling thousands of correlation coefficients to ask, finally: does any specific combination of brain region and frequency band actually track relaxation?

EEG & Frequency Bands

Brain waves sliced into named speed ranges
EEG measures the electrical chatter of cortex through scalp electrodes. That signal is decomposed into bands by frequency: delta (<4 Hz, deep sleep), theta (4–8 Hz, drowsy or meditative), alpha (8–13 Hz, relaxed wakefulness), beta (13–30 Hz, active thought), gamma (>30 Hz, fine-grained attention). Each band reflects different neural populations doing different things. "Brain wave" is shorthand — they're not really waves, they're rhythmic oscillations of cortical excitability.

The 10–20 System

A standardised map of where to put electrodes
EEG electrodes have names like Fp1, Fz, C3, Pz, O1. The letters say which lobe — Frontal, Central, Parietal, Occipital, Temporal. The numbers say which hemisphere — odd = left, even = right, z = midline. This standardisation lets researchers compare studies across labs. In this meta-analysis, the authors group all electrodes into six regional categories so they can ask: does it matter where you measure?

Reference Index of Relaxation

What you compare brain waves against
Because there's no gold-standard "relaxation meter," researchers use proxies — called reference indices. They fall in three families. ECG-related: heart rate, RR-interval variability, LF/HF ratio. Other physiological: blood pressure, skin conductance, saliva cortisol. Questionnaires: self-reported relaxation scales like POMS, the Amsterdam Resting-State Questionnaire, or a visual analog scale. Different indices capture different facets of "feeling relaxed."

Meta-analysis & Fisher's z

Pooling correlations across many studies
Each study reports a correlation coefficient (r) between an EEG measure and a relaxation index. To pool them, the authors apply Fisher's z-transformation — a math trick that converts skewed r values into something roughly normal that can be averaged across studies. A random-effects model then estimates the "true" average effect, with a confidence interval. If the interval crosses zero, the correlation isn't statistically significant.

Reference: The 5 Bands, From First Principles

What each band actually is

The five bands aren't arbitrary names. Each one is produced by a different group of brain cells, working in a different way — that's why each one shows up during a different mental state. Two versions of the same table below: flip between them as you learn.

Band What's actually happening in the brain Why it's faster (or slower) than the next band When you see it — and why
Delta0.5–4 Hz From: cortex-wide — the cortical sheet itself sets the slow rhythm, with the thalamus (a deep brain hub) and brainstem arousal systems holding the tempo low. Huge groups of brain cells switch on and off together in slow waves — they fire briefly, then go quiet, then fire again. It's only possible when the arousal systems that normally keep you awake have powered down. It's the slowest band because it needs millions of cells across wide stretches of cortex to fall silent at the same time. That kind of mass coordination just takes longer. Deep dreamless sleep, and general anaesthesia. Delta is the signature of "consciousness is offline." Sleep doctors literally use it to score how deep someone's sleep is.
Theta4–8 Hz From: two distinct sources — (1) the hippocampus, the brain's memory hub, driven by a small pacemaker just next to it; (2) the front-middle of the cortex, right above your forehead, when you're concentrating hard. A small pacemaker region next to the hippocampus drives it to pulse rhythmically. The frontal source kicks in when you're focusing internally — holding things in mind, doing mental math, paying attention to a memory. Faster than delta because it's driven by a small dedicated pacemaker, not a global slowdown. Slower than alpha because the cells doing the pacing fire at around 5–7 times per second. Drowsiness, deep focus on something internal, and active memory work. Frontal theta goes up when you're holding things in working memory. Hippocampal theta organizes the timing that lets the brain lock in new memories.
Wait — isn't working memory more intensive than concentrating, perceiving, or moving?

Different rhythms ≠ different intensities. Each band reflects the kind of computation, not how hard the brain is working.

  • Theta (working memory) = scheduling. Each ~150 ms theta cycle is one "slot" where a held item gets refreshed. ~4–7 slots per second is all that rotation needs — so the rhythm is slow.
  • Beta (active concentration, motor control) = suppression + steady coordination. ~20 Hz lets it react to incoming distractions and hold motor commands in real time. Slower beta couldn't keep up.
  • Gamma (conscious perception, fine attention) = binding. Combining colour, shape, and motion into one perceived object happens on a millisecond scale — the physical limit of perceptual assembly.

And the bands stack. Solving 17 × 23 in your head runs theta (holds the digits), beta (keeps you focused), and gamma (encodes which digit is which, nested inside each theta slot) — all at once. The slow band isn't "lighter work"; it's the carrier that holds the fast bands together. (See the orange callout below the table for the full picture, including theta-gamma coupling.)
Alpha8–13 Hz From: the thalamus (deep central hub) pulsing the back of the cortex — the visual and parietal areas, near the back of your scalp. That's why alpha is strongest at electrodes over the back of the head. The thalamus pulses the cortex at about 10 beats per second. Each pulse briefly tells the sensory regions of the cortex to "hush." When you close your eyes, this rhythm gets stronger; when you open them, it almost vanishes. Alpha has its own dedicated pacemaker (the thalamus) that the bands on either side don't share. That's why it shuts off the moment sensory input hits — the pacemaker is interruptible. Eyes closed, relaxed, mind idling. Best read as the brain actively shushing the channels it isn't using, so the channels it is using can work clearly. This is the band the meta-analysis finds linked to relaxation.
So when alpha "shuts off" — does the pacemaker actually stop, or is it just being ignored by another part of the brain?

The pacemaker itself stops generating the rhythm. It's not that the thalamus keeps pulsing and something else drowns it out — the rhythm ceases at the source.

Here's the picture. The cells in the thalamus that make alpha can sit in one of two states:

  • Resting state (alpha on): the cells are slightly "below threshold" — quiet enough that a special set of channels (called T-type calcium channels) can reset and fire. When they fire, they do so in rhythmic bursts at ~10 times per second. That bursting is the alpha rhythm.
  • Active state (alpha off): something nudges those cells slightly more excited — pushes their voltage up a notch. The T-type channels can no longer reset, so the bursting stops. The cells now fire continuously, passing sensory information through smoothly instead of rhythmically gating it on and off.

So "alpha block" is the thalamus flipping from rhythm mode to relay mode. The rhythm doesn't get covered up — the machinery that generates it has changed state.

What flips the switch? Three things, all of which excite those thalamic cells:

  • Opening your eyes / a loud sound — incoming sensory signals arrive at the thalamus and start exciting the cells directly.
  • "Waking up" chemicals — when you become alert, your brainstem releases acetylcholine and noradrenaline. These spread into the thalamus and depolarize the relay cells, ending the rhythm.
  • Just deciding to pay attention — your prefrontal and parietal cortex send signals back down to the thalamus that do the same thing. This is why alpha collapses even without any sensory change, if you simply start focusing.

The cortex then loses its synchronized 10 Hz rhythm too — but that's a downstream consequence of the thalamus changing state, not a separate event. Bottom line: alpha block = pacemaker switches mode, not pacemaker overridden.

Beta13–30 Hz From: local circuits in the sensorimotor strip (top of your head, running ear to ear) and the front of the cortex. Motor beta also involves the basal ganglia, deeper in the brain. Small groups of "go" cells and "stop" cells in close balance fire back and forth quickly. Every patch of cortex makes its own beta — there's no single pacemaker. Faster than alpha because the local stop-cells fire about twice as fast. More local than alpha because there's no central pacemaker — every patch of cortex makes its own beta. Active focus, motor control, "keep things the way they are." Beta goes up when you're concentrating on a task or holding a posture, and drops the instant you move or change strategy.
Gamma30–100+ Hz From: small patches of cortex anywhere active processing is happening — gamma is born wherever the work is being done. No central pacemaker, no special region. Very fast "stop" cells gate the firing of nearby "go" cells in tiny 25–40 millisecond windows. The result is millisecond-precise synchrony in small patches of cortex. It's the fastest band because the stop-cells driving it recover from each pulse in only ~10 ms. Always local and short-lived — gamma comes in brief bursts, not steady rhythms. Conscious perception, fine attention, "binding" features into objects. Gamma bursts knit together the brain cells that represent one object or one thought. Weak or noisy gamma is implicated in schizophrenia and Alzheimer's.
Is this just flow state — or the closest thing to it?

Close, but flow isn't a "gamma state." Flow is a combination of band changes, not a single band. The leading neuroscientific account is Arne Dietrich's transient hypofrontality model — during flow, the prefrontal cortex partially powers down, which is what produces the hallmark "no inner critic, no sense of self, time disappears" feeling.

What gets measured during flow:

  • Reduced frontal beta — less of the "monitor, plan, evaluate" signal. This is the prefrontal "quieting."
  • Increased frontal theta + alpha — a focused-but-quiet attention regime, not the high-strain concentration of beta.
  • Strong local gamma in task-relevant areas — the moment-to-moment binding feels seamless because each perceptual "click" of action and feedback is locking in cleanly. This is the part your selection is pointing at.

So: gamma is necessary for the "everything-just-clicks" feel of flow, but not sufficient. You can have strong gamma without flow (any focused perceptual task), and you need the prefrontal quieting + a theta/alpha backbone to get the full flow experience. Flow ≈ quiet prefrontal + theta/alpha carrier + clean local gamma, all at once.

Caveat: flow's EEG signature is still actively debated — sample sizes are small, lab tasks don't fully reproduce real-world flow, and findings replicate unevenly. The hypofrontality framework is the most popular synthesis but is not settled fact.

So how does alpha block happen — does the pacemaker stop, or does another part of the brain override it?

The pacemaker stops generating the rhythm. Alpha isn't "drowned out" or "ignored downstream" — the oscillator itself switches off at the source. Here's the mechanism:

Thalamocortical relay neurons (in pulvinar, LGN, and other sensory thalamic nuclei) have two firing modes, determined by their membrane voltage:

  • Burst mode (eyes closed, relaxed): the relay cells sit hyperpolarized. This deinactivates their T-type Ca²⁺ channels, which fire rhythmic bursts at ~10 Hz. Each burst inhibits cortex via the thalamic reticular nucleus (TRN). That alternation is the alpha rhythm.
  • Tonic mode (eyes open, attending): something depolarizes the relay cells. T-channels become inactivated, the burst rhythm disappears, and the cells start firing continuously — faithfully relaying whatever sensory input arrives, instead of rhythmically gating cortex on/off.

So "alpha block" = the relay neurons crossing the voltage threshold from burst mode to tonic mode. The 10 Hz oscillation isn't suppressed by a stronger signal; the circuit that produces it is no longer in its oscillating regime.

What pushes them across that threshold? Two convergent inputs, both depolarizing:

  • Bottom-up sensory: opening your eyes → retinal volley → LGN. The arriving glutamatergic drive itself starts depolarizing the relay cells.
  • Ascending neuromodulation: arousal switches on cholinergic projections from the basal forebrain and noradrenergic projections from the locus coeruleus. ACh and NE directly depolarize relay neurons, inactivating T-channels.
  • Top-down attention: prefrontal and parietal cortex can do the same thing via corticothalamic feedback. This is why just deciding to pay attention (no sensory change required) also collapses alpha.

The cortex then "desynchronizes" — its neurons start firing at varying phases — but that's a consequence of the thalamus no longer arriving in rhythmic packets, not the primary event. Sources: Steriade, McCormick & Sejnowski 1993 (Science) for the burst/tonic switch; Lőrincz et al. 2009 for the pulvinar's role in alpha; Klimesch 2012 for the attention-driven version.
Beta13–30 Hz From: local sensorimotor cortex (Rolandic strip) and prefrontal microcircuits. Motor beta also has a corticostriatal / basal ganglia component. Local microcircuits — pyramidal cells and fast-spiking parvalbumin interneurons in a tight excitation/inhibition balance. Higher frequency than alpha because the underlying interneuron firing is faster (~20 Hz); more local than alpha because it doesn't depend on thalamic pacing, just on cortical E/I balance. Active concentration, motor planning, status-quo maintenance. Beta increases when you're holding a posture, focusing on an external task, or maintaining the current cognitive set; it suppresses when movement begins.
Gamma30–100+ Hz From: local cortical microcircuits anywhere active processing is happening — any cortical patch where PV+ interneurons gate active pyramidal-cell firing. No central pacemaker; gamma is generated locally by the same circuit doing the computation. The PING mechanism: PV+ interneurons impose 25–40 ms windows of inhibition on pyramidal cells, producing tight synchronization on millisecond timescales. Fastest band because it tracks the membrane time constant of PV+ interneurons (~10 ms); fundamentally local and transient, unlike the more global lower bands. Feature binding, conscious perception, fine-grained attention. Gamma bursts coordinate the cell assemblies that represent a single object or thought; impaired gamma is implicated in schizophrenia and Alzheimer's.

Common misconception: faster Hz ≠ more "intensive" cognition.

It's tempting to read the table top-to-bottom as a difficulty ladder — sleep at the bottom, "real thinking" at the top. That's the wrong mental model. Frequency reflects the timescale of the operation the brain is running, not how hard the brain is working. Each band corresponds to a different kind of operation, and the operation dictates the speed.

Theta (working memory) is slow because its job is scheduling: each ~150 ms theta cycle creates a "slot" where one item being held in mind gets refreshed. Holding 4 items in working memory means cycling through 4 slots per theta wave — the brain literally rehearses them in turn, and 4 refreshes per second is enough to keep them all alive.

Beta (active concentration) is faster because its job is different: suppressing distraction in real time. When you're focused on a task, beta is the brain's "stay on this, ignore everything else" broadcast. It has to be fast (~50 ms responsiveness) because new distractions show up continuously — competing thoughts, sounds, body sensations — and the suppression signal has to react before they hijack you. Slow beta wouldn't be able to keep up with the incoming noise.

Gamma (conscious perception) is fastest because its job is binding: combining color, shape, and motion into one perceived object happens on a millisecond timescale — the limit of how fast a single perceptual moment can be assembled.

So "holding things in memory" is not a lighter version of "concentrating." They're entirely different operations: storage vs. suppression. They have different timescales because they're solving different problems.

And critically: these bands stack, they don't replace each other. When you're doing mental math — say, multiplying 17 × 23 in your head — your brain is running theta (to hold the digits and intermediate result), beta (to keep you focused on the task and ignore distractions), and gamma (nested inside each theta slot, encoding the specific digit being held) all at the same time. The phenomenon of gamma riding on top of theta is called theta-gamma coupling, and it's how the brain stores multiple items at once without them blurring together. The bands aren't competing for "which is harder" — they're cooperating on different parts of the same task.

Why this matters for the meta-analysis. Because each band is produced by different machinery, the fact that only alpha tracks relaxation actually makes sense. Alpha's pacemaker (the thalamus) is the part of the brain that calms down when your parasympathetic nervous system takes over — the same nervous-system switch that "relaxation indices" like heart-rate variability are measuring. The other bands have nothing to do with that switch, so it's not surprising they didn't correlate.

Quick clarifier: yes — every band exists everywhere on the scalp, but with different strengths and different meanings.

The meta-analysis didn't measure "one alpha number per person." It measured alpha, theta, beta, gamma at each of 19 electrodes — so each participant produced 19 alpha values, 19 theta values, and so on. The question wasn't "is there alpha?" (there always is) but "does the alpha here correlate with relaxation?"

Why this matters: the same band can mean different things depending on where it's recorded, because different cortical regions are wired to different generators.

  • Posterior alpha (over occipital/parietal cortex, electrodes Oz/Pz/O1/O2) — the classic "eyes-closed" alpha driven by the thalamic pacemaker. This is the one that tracks relaxation.
  • Frontal alpha (over forehead, Fp1/Fp2/F3/F4) — much smaller in amplitude, and what's there reflects a different signal entirely. Frontal alpha asymmetry (left vs. right) is studied for emotional valence and approach/withdrawal motivation, not relaxation.
  • Central beta (sensorimotor strip, C3/C4) — tied to motor planning. Frontal beta — tied to attention and cognitive control. Same band, different jobs.
  • Frontal-midline theta (Fz) — working-memory load. Hippocampal theta — memory consolidation. Two completely different generators, both labelled "theta."

So when you click around the head map below, you're not seeing "different brains" — you're seeing different physiological generators that all happen to produce signals in the same frequency band. The label "alpha" is geographically promiscuous; the meaning is location-specific.

What "r" and "95% CI" actually mean here — in plain terms.

r is the correlation coefficient. It runs from −1 to +1 and answers: "across people, when EEG measure X goes up, does the relaxation index Y go up too?"

  • r = +1: perfect lockstep — high X always means high Y.
  • r = 0: no linear relationship — knowing X tells you nothing about Y.
  • r = −1: perfect anti-correlation — high X always means low Y.

In this paper's context the signs are pre-aligned — the authors flipped indices that decrease with relaxation (LF/HF ratio, heart rate, blood pressure) so that, for every measure, a higher number = more relaxed. So a positive r means "as alpha power goes up, the participant looks/feels more relaxed." A negative r would mean alpha goes up as they become less relaxed.

How big is r = 0.32? By Cohen's conventional benchmarks: |r| ≈ 0.1 = small, |r| ≈ 0.3 = medium, |r| ≈ 0.5 = large. So the top finding (occipital alpha × questionnaire, r = 0.32) is a medium effect — real, but not enormous. Concretely, r = 0.32 means alpha power explains ~10% of the variance in self-reported relaxation (r² = 0.10). The other 90% is everything else: mood, caffeine, sleep, individual differences, electrode placement noise. So "occipital alpha tracks felt relaxation" is true and replicable — but it's not a tight one-to-one readout.

The 95% CI is the uncertainty around r. It's the range of true r values that are consistent with the data the meta-analysis pooled. "r = 0.32 [0.05, 0.60]" means: given all the studies combined, the actual underlying correlation is plausibly anywhere from 0.05 (barely there) to 0.60 (quite strong) — we just can't pin it down better than that with the data available.

  • If the CI does NOT cross zero (e.g. [0.05, 0.60]) → we can rule out "no correlation" at the 95% confidence level. This is what "statistically significant" means in this table.
  • If the CI DOES cross zero (e.g. [−0.02, 0.28]) → the data are compatible with the true correlation being literally zero, so we can't claim a real relationship — even if the point estimate looks positive. This is the entire reason the naive "alpha = relaxed" story (rank 9) falls apart.
  • How wide the CI is tells you how much data you had. [0.08, 0.40] (5 studies, N=128) is moderately precise. [−7.09, 6.56] (2 studies, N=25) is so wide it's uninformative — those rows are essentially "we have no idea."

The pooling trick: the raw r's across studies are converted to Fisher z-transformed values (z = arctanh(r)) before averaging. This is because r values bunch up near ±1 in an awkward way that breaks ordinary averaging; the z-transform stretches them out so they can be averaged with proper error estimates, then converted back. A random-effects model is used (rather than fixed-effects), which assumes each study is estimating its own slightly different true r — a more conservative assumption that produces wider, more honest CIs when studies disagree with each other (Q statistic captures that disagreement).

One important caveat — correlation isn't causation. Even the three significant rows only say "these vary together across participants/studies." They don't prove that increasing alpha makes you more relaxed, or that being relaxed produces alpha. Both could be downstream of a third thing (eyes-closed posture, the parasympathetic nervous system switching on, mind-wandering state). The paper's "future directions" section explicitly calls for tACS / neurofeedback experiments to test the causal direction — which is what would settle whether targeting alpha is actually a useful intervention or just an incidental marker.

The Method

From 4,194 papers to 31, in five sieves

The authors followed the PRISMA 2020 protocol — the formal standard for systematic reviews — and pre-registered most decisions before reading any results. Two independent reviewers screened titles, abstracts, and full texts. Disagreements went to a third reviewer. Bias was scored on six dimensions (selection, performance, detection, attrition, reporting, other) and indirectness on four more.

The Three Zones

Three specific brain-and-measure combinations actually work

After dicing the data along channel region and reference-index family, exactly three combinations emerged as reliably positive — and none of them overlap. The same brain region pairs with different ground-truth measures depending on where it sits. This is what makes relaxation look multifaceted rather than unitary.

Frontal alpha
↔ All reference indices
r = 0.17
95% CI [0.02, 0.32] · 7 studies, N=145
Frontal cortex is implicated in top-down attention control. Relaxation may partly recruit that control system to dampen task-relevant processing — so frontal alpha rises across any way you operationalise being relaxed.
Central alpha
↔ ECG-related (heart) measures
r = 0.24
95% CI [0.08, 0.40] · 5 studies, N=128
The central scalp sits over sensorimotor cortex, home to the Rolandic rhythm. When you stop planning movement, central alpha rises — and the parasympathetic nervous system shifts simultaneously, which is exactly what ECG measures pick up.
Occipital alpha
↔ Questionnaires (felt relaxation)
r = 0.32
95% CI [0.05, 0.60] · 4 studies, N=134
Occipital cortex is visual cortex. Eyes-closed rest is the textbook trigger of strong occipital alpha. Subjective feeling of "I am relaxed" tracks it well — but objective heart-rate measures don't, suggesting visual cortex idling is felt but not necessarily reflected in autonomic tone.
The reframe: Relaxation isn't a single neural state. It's at least three loosely-coupled processes — attentional, motor, and visual — each leaving its own alpha signature, each best tracked by a different ground-truth measure. The "alpha = relaxed" folk theory was right that alpha matters, wrong that it's one thing.

Implications

Why this should change how you build a relaxation gadget

The market for consumer EEG devices — Muse, Flowtime, Neurosity, the alphabet soup of meditation headbands — runs on the assumption that you can put one or two sensors anywhere on the head and read out a "calm" score. This meta-analysis says: only if you pick the right pair of (sensor location, reference measure) does that score correspond to anything real.

🎧

If you build a consumer headband…

Match the sensor to the claim
A device that claims to measure felt relaxation (the experience) should target occipital alpha. A device that claims to track physiological calm (heart rate, ANS) should target central alpha and pair it with PPG. A device that wants a single "relax score" needs frontal alpha — the most general but weakest of the three signals.

Limits of the evidence

What this paper doesn't settle

The authors are unusually honest about the boundaries of their result. A few things still missing from the literature:

  • Causality is open. Correlation between alpha and relaxation doesn't tell us whether alpha causes the relaxed state or merely reflects it. Targeted stimulation studies (TMS, tACS) are needed.
  • No standard for "relaxation." Every reference index measures something slightly different. The whole field is essentially making bets on which proxy is closest to the ground truth — and there's no agreement.
  • Frequency band ratios were excluded. Things like α/β power ratio — used in many studies — had too few replications to meta-analyse. Splitting alpha into low vs. high sub-bands also remains unexplored at scale.
  • Publication bias. Egger's test flagged significant bias in the theta and beta bands. The alpha band funnel plot looked clean, but the field's tendency to publish positive findings can't be ruled out entirely.
  • Few preregistered studies. 37 of 54 studies could not be assessed for selective reporting because they weren't preregistered. Risk of bias was rated "unclear" by default.

The whole paper in 5 minutes 🧠💥

No jargon. No equations. Just the story.

Step 1. Stick electrodes on someone's head. They pick up tiny electrical waves the brain makes.

Step 2. The waves come in different speeds. The medium-slow ones — about 10 wiggles per second — are called alpha waves.

Step 3. For 100 years, people have said: lots of alpha = you are chilling out.

Analogy
Imagine someone walking into a room and announcing "the lights are flickering — therefore the bulb is broken." Maybe! But maybe the wiring is loose. Or the switch. Or the building's power. You can't tell from the flicker alone what part of the system is misbehaving. That's been the problem with alpha and relaxation: a vague global claim that doesn't say where, or compared to what.

So five scientists rolled up their sleeves and read every study ever published on alpha-and-relaxation. Well, almost — 4,194 candidates, 54 that survived their quality filters, 31 that had numbers good enough to combine.

📚They gathered every study where someone wore EEG and someone also measured relaxation somehow.
🧮They merged the numbers across studies with a statistical machine called a meta-analysis.
🗺️Then they sliced the result by where on the head the electrodes were, and by what relaxation actually means in each study.

Here's what they found.

If you just take all the alpha studies and average them, the correlation between alpha and relaxation is… kind of meh. Maybe positive, but the error bars touch zero. The popular "alpha = chill" story is mostly vibes.

BUT — when they sliced the data by location, three specific spots lit up:

🎯Front of the head + any measure → mild correlation. Front of the brain runs attention; relaxing means dialing attention down.
❤️Middle of the head + heart-rate measures → solid correlation. The middle of the brain runs movement; quieting movement quiets the heart too.
👁️Back of the head + "how relaxed do you feel?" surveys → strongest correlation. The back of the brain handles vision; eyes-closed = vision idling = feels calm.
The big reframe
Relaxation is not one thing. It's at least three different things wearing the same word: your attention calming down, your body calming down, and your felt sense of being at ease. Each one has its own brain signature in its own place. Trying to measure "relaxation" with a single sensor is like trying to measure "hunger" with a single number — possible, but you'll miss most of the picture.
TL;DR

For a century, people thought "alpha brain waves = relaxed." A new meta-analysis of 54 studies says: it's true, but only in three very specific combinations. The front of the brain tracks general relaxation. The middle tracks the body. The back tracks the feeling. There is no one "calm" signal — relaxation is three things, not one.

💡 What this opens up

If relaxation is three signals, not one — here's what we could build next.

Product

A three-channel "calm score" headband

One frontal, one central, one occipital electrode — and a PPG (heart-rate) sensor. Surface three independent scores: cognitive calm, physiological calm, felt calm. Users see when the three diverge — e.g., body is calm but mind is racing — and the device tells them which to address. Existing single-sensor headbands collapse the three into one number, hiding exactly the signal the user needs.

Research

Test the causal direction with tACS

The meta-analysis can only show correlation. Use transcranial alternating current stimulation to externally entrain alpha at each of the three regions separately. Does driving frontal alpha at 10 Hz reduce reported stress? Does driving central alpha at 10 Hz lower heart rate? If the regions truly map to distinct facets of relaxation, the causal arrows should differ in direction and magnitude.

Method

Split alpha into low (8–10 Hz) and high (10–13 Hz)

The authors flag that alpha probably isn't one band but two. Low alpha tracks general arousal; high alpha tracks attention. A follow-up meta-analysis that retains the sub-band split could explain part of the noisy signal in the current pooled estimate — and reveal whether the three regional couplings further differentiate by sub-band.

Clinical

Targeted neurofeedback for insomnia, anxiety, PTSD

Existing alpha neurofeedback protocols train "alpha up" without specifying a region. If insomnia is mostly a vision-cortex-can't-shut-down problem, occipital protocols should help most. If anxiety is a top-down-control problem, frontal should help most. The current literature is too coarse to test this — but the three-zone result gives a clear way to design the next generation of trials.

Pop-sci

Retire the phrase "alpha brain waves" in marketing copy

It promises one thing and means three. Replacing it with "frontal calm," "central calm," and "felt calm" would actually be more accurate and more interesting. The meta-analysis is a chance to update the script — but only if people writing about brain-waves bother to read systematic reviews instead of news releases.

Open question

Why doesn't the occipital signal correlate with heart rate?

The most surprising negative in the paper: occipital alpha tracks subjective relaxation but not ECG. Why would visual-cortex idling feel like calm without affecting autonomic tone? Possibilities: (a) closing one's eyes is the act that drives both occipital alpha and the subjective rating, but doesn't reliably shift heart rate; (b) the felt sense of calm precedes any physiological change by minutes; (c) the questionnaire measures something the body doesn't really track. All are testable.
